## 沉劲 · chén jìn

### The Sinking Force

---

> Heavy as the mountain.

— Traditional saying

---

## The Essence of Chén Jìn

Chén means to sink, to be heavy.

Chén Jìn is the force of inner heaviness. It is not a push downward, but a quality of presence: the body becomes heavy, dense, impossible to lift.

The masters describe it as:

· a continuous descent of energy,

· a surrender to gravity,

· a heaviness that does not come from the muscles but from release.

---

## Fundamental Principles

1\. Heaviness is born of release

The more the body releases, the heavier it becomes. Muscular tension lightens, for it holds energy up high. Release lets energy descend.

2\. The breath sinks below the navel

Chén Jìn demands that the breathing be low, abdominal, calm. A high breath makes the body float. A low breath roots it.

3\. The joints open downward

Shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees: each joint relaxes and opens in the direction of the ground. The skeleton empties itself of all intention to rise.

4\. The opponent feels an impossible weight to move

Chén Jìn does not push the opponent downward. It simply renders the practitioner's body so heavy that the opponent can no longer move it. It is a mountain: it does not attack, yet one cannot lift it.

---

## Final Summary

Chén Jìn is the force of inhabited gravity. It does not weigh deliberately; it surrenders to heaviness. And in that surrender, it becomes heavier than any force could ever lift.

## 磨盘劲 · mò pán jìn

### The Millstone Force

---

> When the millstone turns, the opponent overturns himself.

— Traditional saying

---

## The Essence of Mò Pán Jìn

Mò Pán Jìn is neither a circular push nor a localized rotation of the arms.

The old masters describe it as:

· a circle that engages the entire body,

· a rotation that rises from the ground and passes through the pelvis,

· a force that never leaves its axis,

· and that wears down the opponent's structure without ever striking it.

Mò Pán Jìn does not project outward.

It remains.

And it is what remains that grinds.

---

## Fundamental Principles

1\. The millstone turns; it does not push

The millstone does not push the grain. It turns. The grain places itself between the stones, and the rotation does the rest. One does not seek to displace the opponent; one turns in place, massively, and everything resting upon that rotation gradually loses its supports.

2\. The movement comes from the ground, not the arms

The apparent rotation is only the surface. The true engine is in the feet, the hips, the pelvis, the spine. The arms merely accompany. If the arms turn without the pelvis, the millstone does not exist.

3\. Slowness is the condition of efficacy

Mò Pán Jìn tolerates no haste. A rapid rotation breaks the internal connection, alerts the opponent, transforms the millstone into a mere circular strike. Slow rotation maintains continuity, absorbs resistances, wears away without the other perceiving the exact moment of his loss.

4\. The axis does not move

The millstone possesses a fixed center. In Mò Pán Jìn, the practitioner's center does not shift, the root does not lift, the breath does not rise. If the axis moves, the force disperses. If the axis remains, the rotation becomes irresistible.

5\. The force grinds without destroying

Mò Pán Jìn does not seek brutal rupture. It wears away supports, balance, internal structure. The opponent feels no shock. He feels the ground turning beneath him, his center shifting without reason, his force finding no point of support. Then he falls. Not because he has been overturned, but because the ground under him has changed.

---

## The Levels of Mò Pán Jìn

**Beginner level:** The rotation is still localized. The shoulders or arms initiate the circle. The opponent can resist by rooting.

**Intermediate level:** The pelvis becomes the engine. The rotation passes through the spine. The opponent feels a turning pressure, but can still retreat.

**Advanced level:** The rotation rises from the feet. The entire body becomes a single millstone. The opponent can neither advance, nor retreat, nor turn. He rests upon a plane that moves as a whole. He loses all orientation.

---

## Final Summary

Mò Pán Jìn is not an arm technique. It is not a local twist. It is not a decorative circle. It is a rotation of the entire body, rooted in the ground, directed by the pelvis, carried by an immobile axis, that gradually wears away the opponent's supports and structure.

The true Mò Pán Jìn appears slow, remains massive, shows no violence, yet transforms all opposition into dust.

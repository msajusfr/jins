## 拿劲 · ná jìn

### The Seizing and Controlling Force

**Definition.** Ná Jìn is the force of seizing and controlling. It does not mean grabbing with the hand alone; it means fixing a joint, line, or axis after listening and guiding have revealed a structural weakness.

**Detailed explanation.** A true Ná Jìn begins before the visible hold. The practitioner first listens, follows, and wraps the opponent's line. Only when the joint or axis has lost its freedom does the control become clear. The hand may appear to seize, but the body, waist, and footwork are what make the control effective.

**Biomechanics.** Keep the shoulders low, the elbows alive, and the center connected to the hands. The grip should be directed by the waist and supported by the legs, not isolated in the forearms.

**Drill.** Work slowly from light wrist, elbow, and shoulder contacts. First create a small loss of alignment, then close the line without squeezing too early.

**Martial application.** Use Ná Jìn to control an arm, interrupt a strike, expose the opponent's center, or transition into qinna, projection, or issuing force.

**Common mistakes.** Grabbing too soon, squeezing with the hand, raising the shoulders, and trying to force the joint before the structure has been led into weakness.

**Mastery milestone.** The partner feels that the exit has disappeared before the grip becomes strong.

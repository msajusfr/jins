## 长劲 · cháng jìn

### The Long Force

---

> The river does not strike the stone; it passes around it.

— Traditional saying

---

## The Essence of Cháng Jìn

Cháng means long, continuous, unbroken.

Cháng Jìn is a force that does not stop. It is not a blow; it is a current. It does not seek to destroy in an instant, but to move what it meets through sustained pressure.

The masters describe it as:

· a wave that does not break upon the shore but continues,

· a wind that pushes without ceasing,

· a force that outlasts all resistance.

---

## Fundamental Principles

1\. The force is a current, not a blow

Cháng Jìn does not strike and withdraw. It establishes contact and continues. The opponent does not feel an impact; he feels a pressure that grows, that persists, that does not give him a moment to recover.

2\. Continuity is more important than intensity

A violent blow can be resisted. A continuous force cannot. Cháng Jìn sacrifices peak intensity for uninterrupted presence. It is the drip that wears away the stone.

3\. The body remains filled throughout the emission

Unlike explosive forces that empty the body in an instant, Cháng Jìn maintains fullness. The practitioner does not collapse after emitting; he remains present, ready to continue.

4\. The breath sustains the force

Cháng Jìn is carried by a long, continuous exhalation that does not interrupt. The breath and the force are one.

---

## Final Summary

Cháng Jìn is the patience of energy. It does not strike to destroy, but emits to displace. It knows no end, only a flow that traverses everything it meets.
